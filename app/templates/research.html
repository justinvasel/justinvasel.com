Research

My work mainly involves the detection of galactic supernova neutrinos. That's a little misleading, because we haven't had any supernova neutrinos to detect since 1987 (which was in general a great year, I might add). But, someday sooner or later another galactic supernova will happen and we damn well better be ready to catch those neutrinos as they zip through our detectors. So that's my job.

Additionally, I have done a lot of work in the data acquisition side of things. I specialize in designing, improving, and maintaining monitoring systems, and I am one of just a few experts on the timing system at the NOvA experiment.

My life as a researcher began in 2009 as a physics undergraduate studying the H\(\alpha\) emitting zones of the star Spica. I've always had a soft spot for astrophysics, so I decided to stick with it when I began pursuing my masters degree in physics at the University of Minnesota Duluth (UMD). "Fine, I'll do physics," I said to no one in particular. "But, it better have something to do with astronomy."

"Okay," said my research adviser, Dr. Alec Habig (I'm paraphrasing). So I started work at the Helium and Lead Observatory (HALO), a dedicated supernova neutrino detector 6,800 feet below ground at SNOLAB in Ontario.

After graduating from UMD with my masters, I set up shop in Indiana University's physics department for another helping of graduate studies. Somehow, I managed to survive a second encounter with Jackson's E&M and began my research with the NOvA experiment under the advisement of Dr. Mark Messier.
